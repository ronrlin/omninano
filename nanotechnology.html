Think nano. Think Big.

Nanotechnology, or the science of the small, will have a big impact on your life. In fact, it already has. You are likely reading this on a computer that uses microchips that incorporate nanotechnology!

Nanotechnology is many things. But fundamentally, nanotechnology is science and technology that relies on nanometer-scale properties of materials, molecules and more.

For computers, nanotechnology will mean smaller microchips, and perhaps even flexible and durable displays. For medicine, nanotechnology will prolong life expectancy, by increasingly allowing clinicians to target diseases very precisely, minimizing harm to patients and improving health outcomes. For the environment, nanotechnology might mean a greener and more sustainable future.

For the reasons listed above, now is the time to start educating American youth about nanotechnology. By building awareness now, it might be possible to seed the next generation of scientists and engineers who advance many of these nanotechnology applications.

The next Industrial Revolution

A Nanotechnology Revolution is already in motion, and this revolution will change the world in dramatic fashion. Radical changes are coming to the ways in which medicine is practiced, energy is generated, and commercial goods are produced.

The 19th century American Industrial Revolution brought profound changes to the U.S. economy, as well as to the social and cultural structure of the country - for example, the advent of the assembly line sparked a boom in manufacturing, while the new factory class of workers spurred urbanization. The Nanotechnology Revolution is similarly expected to bring another round of fundamental changes to the U.S. economy and society.

But what's different about nanotechnology?

Human civilization has always relied on producing materials using "bulk processes". In fact, a "classical" science curriculum in chemistry and biology presents only a bulk process, top-down education. This means that we normally rely on moving billions and trillions of molecules or atoms around in order to produce chemicals, microchips or medicines. Nanotechnology involves assembling materials from the bottom up, implying dramatic improvements in yields, efficiency and likely lowering the costs to produce many goods.

In contrast to 19th century industrial revolution, the nanotechnology revolution will need scientists and innovators working to manipulate matter at the nanoscale. Current STEM curricula focus on bulk processes, which is universally considered a necessary foundation for any scientist to begin from. However, while this foundation is critical, building awareness of nanotechnology is critical to buiding increasing numbers of students into nanotechnology programs, ensuring that the United States preserves its legacy at the forefront of advances in the 20th century.
